# PaperMeister Pricing and Go-To-Market Memo

**Date:** 2026-04-03  
**Status:** Draft  
**Scope:** Early commercialization strategy for validating willingness to pay before full product completion

## 1. Purpose

This memo is for answering one practical question:

**if PaperMeister were to be sold, who would pay, for what, at what price, and through what path?**

The goal is not to finalize a business model yet.
The goal is to validate whether a real paying market exists before large additional build-out.

## 2. Core Commercial Thesis

PaperMeister should not be positioned as:

- another reference manager
- another PDF reader
- another generic AI research app

It should be positioned as:

**a system that turns scattered paper libraries into a searchable, maintainable research corpus without forcing users to abandon their existing tools**

That positioning matters because it defines why someone would pay.

## 3. Who Pays

There are three realistic customer layers.

## 3.1 Individual Researchers

### Why they might buy

- they have many PDFs scattered across folders and reference tools
- they frequently fail to rediscover papers they already own
- they want OCR-backed full-text search over their real archive
- they prefer local control over cloud-only AI tools

### Strength

- easy to reach
- fast feedback
- good for early validation

### Weakness

- low budget
- strong price sensitivity
- many will compare against free tools

## 3.2 Labs and Research Groups

### Why they might buy

- papers are distributed across students, postdocs, folders, and Zotero libraries
- group knowledge is fragmented
- new members cannot easily inherit the lab’s literature base
- important PDFs exist but are not operationally searchable as a shared corpus

### Strength

- stronger willingness to pay
- clearer operational pain
- better fit for multi-source sync and corpus positioning

### Weakness

- decision-making is slower
- setup and trust matter more
- support expectations are higher

## 3.3 Institutions and Specialized Teams

Examples:

- university research centers
- museums
- corporate R&D teams
- domain archives

### Why they might buy

- they need internal literature infrastructure
- they care about long-lived corpus assets
- they may need installation help, migration, or customization

### Strength

- highest contract value
- strong use case if domain fit is real

### Weakness

- long sales cycle
- procurement complexity
- support and deployment burden

## 4. Best Initial Beachhead

The most realistic first paying segment is:

**small research labs with accumulated PDFs, mixed bibliography habits, and a real need to search and preserve literature as a shared asset**

This is better than leading with institutions because:

- sales cycle is shorter
- pain is more immediate
- founder-led selling is realistic
- product can remain local-first and simple

## 5. What They Pay For

People will not primarily pay for “OCR” alone.

They will pay for:

- rescuing hard-to-use paper archives
- reducing time lost to rediscovery
- making scattered sources searchable together
- preserving lab literature as an operable asset
- enabling future structured analysis on top of that corpus

The product value is:

**corpus intelligence built on top of existing libraries**

## 6. Pricing Model Options

Early pricing should be simple.

## 6.1 Individual Plan

Good for early adopters and independent researchers.

Possible form:

- one-time local license
- or low annual subscription

Indicative range:

- one-time: USD 79-149
- annual: USD 48-96

### Notes

This tier should stay simple and mostly self-serve.

## 6.2 Lab Plan

Best target for initial revenue.

Possible form:

- lab annual subscription
- per-lab license rather than per-seat complexity at the start

Indicative range:

- USD 600-2,000 per lab per year

This depends on:

- shared workflows
- number of active users
- onboarding/support level
- OCR cost handling

### Notes

Labs buy convenience, continuity, and shared corpus value, not just a desktop tool.

## 6.3 Institutional / Custom Plan

For organizations needing deployment help, custom support, or domain adaptation.

Possible form:

- annual contract
- setup fee plus support fee

Indicative range:

- USD 5,000+ annually

### Notes

This should be priced manually, not published at first.

## 7. OCR Cost Strategy

OCR cost is structurally important.

The product should not quietly absorb unlimited OCR usage.

Early options:

- customer brings their own OCR credentials
- bundled OCR usage with caps
- usage-based surcharge for heavy OCR

The safest early policy is:

**let advanced users bring their own OCR key, and avoid open-ended OCR liability**

This prevents pricing from collapsing under heavy use.

## 8. Packaging Strategy

An early package structure could look like this.

## Personal

- local use
- multi-source import
- OCR-backed search
- limited support

## Lab

- multiple users or shared workflow orientation
- source sync management
- better support
- corpus continuity for the group

## Institutional

- deployment help
- advanced support
- custom onboarding
- possible domain customization later

## 9. Go-To-Market Principle

The first goal is not scale.

The first goal is:

**prove that specific users with a specific pain will commit to a paid pilot**

This is a customer discovery and trust-building process, not a broad launch.

## 10. Messaging Direction

Avoid generic AI language.

Lead with a concrete pain:

- scattered PDFs
- incomplete reference managers
- lost institutional memory
- unsearchable scan archives

Better messaging examples:

- “Turn your scattered paper archive into a searchable research corpus.”
- “Use Zotero, EndNote, and PDF folders together instead of choosing one.”
- “Make your lab’s literature searchable, reusable, and continuously updated.”

## 11. Early Marketing Channels

The best early channels are narrow and trust-based.

Recommended:

- direct outreach to labs
- founder-led conversations with researchers
- field-specific communities
- research Twitter/X, Mastodon, Reddit, mailing lists where appropriate
- conference hallway demos or small workshops
- short demo videos showing before/after workflow

Less useful early:

- broad paid ads
- generic AI-product launch campaigns
- polished scale marketing before customer discovery

## 12. Validation Sequence

Recommended order:

1. define one target segment narrowly
2. test pain statement in conversations
3. show mockup or demo
4. ask for pilot commitment
5. ask for paid pilot if interest is real
6. refine pricing after concrete conversations

The most important signal is not “sounds useful.”
It is:

- willingness to schedule follow-up
- willingness to trial with real data
- willingness to pay for pilot use

## 13. Key Objections to Prepare For

Likely objections:

- “Why not just use Zotero?”
- “Why not just use desktop search?”
- “Will this touch or corrupt my existing library?”
- “Where does the OCR run?”
- “What about unpublished or licensed PDFs?”
- “How much setup does this take?”

Go-to-market material should answer these clearly.

## 14. Recommended Near-Term Offer

The most realistic early commercial offer is:

**founder-led paid pilot for small research labs with scattered literature sources**

Suggested pilot framing:

- limited number of labs
- local-first deployment
- import from current sources
- OCR-backed search corpus
- direct onboarding support
- discounted early pricing in exchange for feedback

## 15. Summary

PaperMeister’s early business path should be:

- start with labs, not mass-market consumers
- sell solved pain, not abstract AI capability
- keep pricing simple
- control OCR cost exposure
- use pilots to validate willingness to pay before major expansion

The immediate goal is not “launch broadly.”
The immediate goal is:

**find a narrow group that already has the problem badly enough to pay to solve it now**
