# PaperMeister Customer Discovery Plan

**Date:** 2026-04-03  
**Status:** Draft  
**Scope:** Validate demand and willingness to pay before major product expansion

## 1. Goal

The purpose of customer discovery is not to hear compliments.

It is to answer:

- who has the sharpest pain
- how they describe that pain in their own words
- what they currently do instead
- whether they would actually adopt and pay for a solution

## 2. Initial Hypothesis

Initial hypothesis:

**small research labs with mixed paper-management habits have a real problem preserving and searching their literature corpus**

Typical condition:

- some papers in Zotero
- old references in EndNote or exported bibliographies
- many PDFs in folders or drives
- scan PDFs or OCR-poor documents
- no unified searchable corpus

## 3. Discovery Segments

Recommended initial segments:

- individual researchers with large PDF archives
- small to mid-sized research labs
- domain-heavy literature users such as paleontology, biology, museum research groups

Primary focus should be labs, with individuals used as a secondary signal source.

## 4. Discovery Questions to Answer

The first round should answer these questions:

- where do they currently store papers
- how many systems are involved
- what fails most often in their current workflow
- how often they cannot rediscover something they already have
- whether scans and low-quality PDFs are part of the problem
- whether this is a personal pain or a lab-level pain
- whether they would try a local-first corpus tool
- whether they would pay for it

## 5. Interview Format

Recommended format:

- 30 to 45 minute conversations
- first 20 minutes about current workflow and pain
- next 10 to 15 minutes showing mockup, concept, or lightweight demo
- final 5 to 10 minutes testing adoption and payment signals

Do not begin by pitching features.
Begin by mapping current behavior.

## 6. Interview Script

## Part 1. Current Workflow

- How do you currently manage papers?
- What role do Zotero, EndNote, folders, or drives play?
- Where do old PDFs usually end up?
- How do you search across your existing literature?
- What kinds of documents are hardest to find again?

## Part 2. Pain and Failure Cases

- Tell me about the last time you knew you had a paper but couldn’t find it efficiently.
- Do scans or OCR-poor PDFs create problems for you?
- Do you have papers that exist in more than one place?
- How easy is it for a new lab member to inherit the lab’s literature collection?
- Does your current setup feel like a searchable corpus or just storage?

## Part 3. Reaction to Concept

- What if your Zotero, old references, and PDF folders could all feed into one searchable corpus?
- What would be most valuable: search, OCR, sync, or shared lab continuity?
- What would make you hesitate to adopt something like this?
- Would you use it personally, or would this be more valuable at the lab level?

## Part 4. Commitment Signals

- Would you try this with your real data?
- Would you want a pilot for your lab?
- If it worked, would this be worth paying for?
- Who would approve that purchase?

## 7. What to Listen For

Good signals:

- concrete examples of lost time
- multi-source fragmentation pain
- repeated manual work
- concern about lab continuity or onboarding
- immediate willingness to test with real data
- asking about rollout, not just features

Weak signals:

- “interesting idea”
- “could be useful someday”
- broad enthusiasm with no example
- refusal to use real data

## 8. Behavioral Signals to Track

Track actions, not only opinions.

Strongest signals:

- agrees to follow-up
- agrees to a pilot
- offers sample data
- asks about timeline
- asks about price
- asks whether colleagues can join

These matter more than positive verbal feedback.

## 9. Pilot Validation Goal

Discovery should lead to one of these outcomes:

- no meaningful pain found
- pain found but low willingness to pay
- strong pain and willingness to trial
- strong pain and willingness to pay for pilot

The target outcome is:

**2 to 5 real pilot candidates who want to test PaperMeister with their own paper collections**

## 10. Numbers to Aim For

Practical first pass:

- 10 to 15 researcher interviews
- 5 to 8 lab-oriented conversations
- 3 to 5 follow-up demos
- 2 to 3 serious pilot discussions

This is enough to refine the message and decide whether to push further.

## 11. Discovery Assets Needed

Before interviews, prepare:

- a one-paragraph product description
- one-page overview or leaflet
- 2 to 5 screenshots or mockups
- a short explanation of what is already real versus planned
- a simple pilot offer outline

Use the smallest amount of material needed to trigger specific discussion.

## 12. Risks During Discovery

- pitching too much and listening too little
- attracting people who like AI tools but do not have the pain
- confusing curiosity with purchase intent
- interviewing only friendly contacts
- not asking who would actually approve spending

## 13. Decision Rule After Discovery

After the first interview round, evaluate:

- which segment had the clearest pain
- which phrasing resonated most
- which feature mattered first
- which objections repeated
- whether anyone showed real willingness to pay

Then choose one:

1. proceed with a paid pilot offer
2. narrow the target segment further
3. reposition the product message
4. stop broad expansion and focus on personal-use tooling

## 14. Summary

Customer discovery for PaperMeister should focus on one thing:

**finding users who already feel the pain of scattered, half-searchable literature strongly enough to test and pay for a corpus solution**

The discovery process should prioritize:

- real workflow detail
- real failure cases
- real commitment signals

Not abstract praise.
